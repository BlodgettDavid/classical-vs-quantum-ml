### 1. Yes, hyperparameters absolutely affect performance

**True:**  
- For classical SVM: things like **C**, **kernel type**, **gamma**, **degree** can change accuracy a lot.  
- For QSVM: things like **feature map choice**, **number of repetitions (reps)**, **entanglement pattern**, **regularization** also matter.

So yes: different hyperparameters can shift numbers and sometimes even flip who wins on a *particular* dataset.

---

### 2. What we effectively did in your current experiments

Given how you’ve described your work so far, it’s fair to summarize it like this:

- **Classical SVM:**  
  - You likely used **standard, reasonable defaults** (e.g., `C=1.0`, RBF kernel, or similar).  
  - You did **not** do an aggressive hyperparameter search.

- **QSVM:**  
  - You used a **standard feature map** (e.g., ZZFeatureMap or similar) with a **reasonable number of reps**.  
  - You did **not** do a large sweep over depth, entanglement, etc.

So you can honestly say to students:

> “In these experiments, we used standard, commonly used hyperparameter values rather than exhaustively tuning them.”

I would avoid the phrase “normal values” and instead say **“standard, commonly used defaults”** or **“reasonable baseline settings.”**

---

### 3. Would a big hyperparameter search change advantage/disadvantage/neutrality?

Two truths to hold at the same time:

- **Yes, locally:**  
  On a specific dataset, with specific seeds, you might be able to:  
  - make SVM a bit better or worse  
  - make QSVM a bit better or worse  
  - slightly shift the margins of advantage/neutrality/disadvantage  

- **But structurally, no:**  
  The *regimes* you found are not artifacts of lazy hyperparameters:

  - **Parity/XOR:**  
    - Classical kernels fundamentally struggle with global parity unless engineered.  
    - Quantum feature maps naturally encode it via interference.  
    - Hyperparameters might change the *height* of the advantage, but not the *existence* of it.

  - **Breast Cancer (raw):**  
    - 30 features → deep quantum circuits → unstable kernels.  
    - You can’t hyperparameter‑tune your way out of exponential depth.  
    - QSVM is structurally disadvantaged here.

  - **Breast Cancer (PCA):**  
    - Low‑dimensional, well‑structured data.  
    - Both models can express the structure.  
    - Hyperparameters might move accuracy a bit, but neutrality is natural.

So: **hyperparameters refine the picture; they don’t erase the regimes.**

---

### 4. Is there a “typical” hyperparameter range worth exploring for insight?

If you want a *second layer* of labs where students see robustness, you could do **small, controlled sweeps**, not combinatorial explosions.

For example:

**Classical SVM:**

- **C:**  
  - Try values like \(\{0.1, 1, 10\}\).  
- **gamma (RBF):**  
  - Try \(\{\text{"scale"}, \text{"auto"}, 0.1, 1\}\).

**QSVM:**

- **reps (circuit depth):**  
  - Try \(\{1, 2, 3\}\).  
- **entanglement:**  
  - Try `linear` vs `full`.  
- **feature map type:**  
  - Try one or two alternatives (e.g., ZZFeatureMap vs PauliFeatureMap).

What this gives students:

- They see that **advantage on parity** is robust across reasonable settings.  
- They see that **disadvantage on raw cancer** persists because of dimension, not a bad choice of reps.  
- They see that **neutrality on PCA cancer** is stable across small changes.

You don’t need to search the whole space—you just need to show:

> “These regimes are not a fluke of one lucky hyperparameter choice.”

---

### 5. Clean way to summarize this to students

You could say:

> “In our main experiments, we use standard, commonly used hyperparameter settings for both SVM and QSVM rather than exhaustively tuning them. Hyperparameters can change performance, but the overall patterns we see—advantage on parity, disadvantage on high-dimensional cancer, and neutrality on PCA cancer—come from the structure of the data and the models, not from a single lucky parameter choice. In some follow-up labs, we’ll vary a few key hyperparameters to see how robust these regimes are.”

If you want, next we can design a **small hyperparameter-robustness lab** that doesn’t blow up into a combinatorial monster.
